7 Warning Signs Your AC Is About to Fail (Don't Ignore These)

By Huntsville HVAC Pros | Expert HVAC advice for Huntsville, Alabama homeowners.

In Huntsville's summer heat, your AC isn't just a comfort appliance — it's a necessity. An unexpected failure when temperatures hit 95°F puts your family, pets, and even your home's structure at risk. The bad news is that AC systems don't usually fail without warning. The good news: if you know what to look for, you can catch problems before they become emergencies.

Here are the 7 warning signs our technicians see most often before a full AC failure in Huntsville homes.

1. Warm Air Coming from the Vents

This one is obvious but often ignored. If your AC is running but the air from the vents feels warm or less cool than it used to, something is wrong. Common causes:

  • Low refrigerant — often from a slow leak that's gotten worse over months
  • Dirty condenser coils — the outdoor unit can't reject heat into Alabama's already-hot ambient air
  • Compressor beginning to fail — early-stage compressor problems often show as reduced cooling before complete failure

Don't just crank the thermostat lower and hope it catches up. That puts even more stress on a struggling system.

2. Strange Noises — Banging, Squealing, or Rattling

Your AC should be relatively quiet — a gentle hum and whoosh of air. Any of these noises are red flags:

  • Banging or clanking — a loose or broken component inside the compressor or air handler
  • Squealing — a worn or failing fan motor belt, or bearings failing in a motor
  • Rattling — loose screws, debris in the unit, or a failing blower wheel
  • Clicking at startup/shutdown — normal; continuous clicking is a failing relay or electrical component
  • Hissing — could indicate a refrigerant leak — call immediately

3. Your AC Runs Constantly Without Reaching Temperature

On a 90°F Huntsville summer day, your AC will run most of the day — that's normal. But if it never cycles off and your home still feels warm, that's a system struggling to keep up. This can mean low refrigerant, undersized equipment, poor insulation, or a failing compressor. The longer you run it in this state, the more damage accumulates.

4. Unusually High Electric Bills

If your July electric bill is noticeably higher than last year with similar weather and usage patterns, your AC is working harder than it should. Dirty coils, low refrigerant, and worn mechanical components all reduce efficiency — sometimes by 20–30%. An aging system that used to be efficient may now cost you significantly more to run than a new high-efficiency replacement.

5. Frozen Ice on the Lines or Outdoor Unit

If you see ice on the copper refrigerant lines coming from the outdoor unit, or ice on the outdoor unit itself, shut the AC off immediately and call us. A frozen coil blocks airflow completely — your AC will run but produce zero cooling, and you risk burning out the compressor by overworking it against the restriction. Common causes: dirty filter (change it first), low refrigerant, or restricted airflow.

6. Water Pooling Around the Indoor Unit

Some condensation around your air handler is normal. Puddles are not. A clogged condensate drain line is the most common cause — fixable and inexpensive. But an overflowing drain pan can cause significant water damage to your ceiling, walls, and floor. Some systems have a float switch that shuts the AC off before overflow — but not all.

In Alabama's humidity, your AC removes a tremendous amount of moisture from the air (2–3 gallons per hour on humid days). That water needs somewhere to go. If the drain is blocked, it will find somewhere — usually somewhere you don't want.

7. Your System Is Over 12 Years Old and Has Had Multiple Repairs

This isn't a symptom — it's a context that changes how you interpret everything else. The average AC system lasts 15–20 years with good maintenance. But systems that haven't been maintained, or that have had multiple major repairs in the past 3 years, may be telling you it's time.

Use the 5,000 rule: multiply the repair cost by the age of the unit. If the result exceeds $5,000, replacement is usually smarter. And if your system uses R-22 refrigerant (phased out in 2020), factor in the dramatically higher cost of that refrigerant versus replacing with a modern R-410A system.

Act Early, Save Big

The theme across all of these warning signs: the earlier you act, the cheaper it is. A $150 capacitor replacement today prevents a $400 emergency call next month. A $350 refrigerant recharge this spring prevents a $2,000 compressor replacement from running hot all summer.
